Abstract 

While LLM-based Automatic Speech Recog-nition (ASR) achieves high accuracy, its speed is limited by sequential autoregressive decod-ing. Diffusion Language Models (DLMs) of-fer a parallel alternative, yet their decoding strategies remain under-explored in ASR con-texts. This paper analyzes three decoding schemes for DLM-based ASR: fixed-number, static confidence threshold, and dynamic confi-dence threshold. We propose measuring round-wise accuracy using Negative Log-Likelihood-based uncertainty as a proxy for decoding progress. Our results show that both threshold-based strategies significantly outperform fixed-number schemes in accuracy and speed. We attribute this to a property unique to ASR: most tokens reach high confidence early, allowing re-liable ones to be harvested aggressively while leaving only difficult tokens for later rounds. Notably, the static-threshold strategy matches the accuracy of autoregressive decoding while offering superior efficiency. 

1 Introduction 

Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) has advanced through self-supervised pretrain-ing (Baevski et al., 2020; Schneider et al., 2019; Hsu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022), large-scale weakly supervised training (Radford et al., 2023; Pratap et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024), and LLM-based decoder modeling (Grattafiori et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023). However, LLM-based ASR remains slow due to autoregressive (AR) decoding and large parameter counts. Recent work (Wang et al., 2025) has therefore explored Diffusion Language Models (DLMs) (Nie et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025) for ASR, which enable bidirectional-attention-based parallel decoding by

Since token commitment in DLMs relies on heuristic confidence criteria, prior work has ex-plored several strategies: fixed-number decoding, which commits a fixed number of high-confidence tokens; static thresholding, which commits tokens above a fixed confidence threshold; and dynamic thresholding, which adapts the commitment cri-terion by the current confidence distribution (Fu et al., 2025; Ben-Hamu et al.; Nie et al., 2025; Chang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025). However, DLM-based ASR (Wang et al., 2025) has remained centered on fixed-number decoding, leav-ing alternative strategies without systematic eval-uation. We therefore compare these strategies in terms of final accuracy–speed trade-offs and round-wise decoding progress, showing that thresholding strategies are more effective and revealing where decoding time and accuracy gaps arise. Concretely, we analyze decoding progress through prediction reliability and token through-put. We quantify reliability using token-level Neg-ative Log-Likelihood (NLL), using an AR config-uration of the same DLM as a reference because it predicts each token with maximal contextual in-formation and consistently outperforms parallel strategies. We measure throughput by counting newly unmasked tokens in each round, and fur-ther examine per-round confidence distributions to understand how each strategy converts confidence estimates into token-commitment decisions. Our contributions are threefold. First, we sys-tematically compare fixed-number, static-threshold, and dynamic-threshold decoding for DLM-based ASR, showing that thresholding strategies pro-vide a better accuracy–speed trade-off. Second, we analyze round-wise decoding behavior using NLL and token throughput, revealing early high-confidence commitments followed by slower refine-ment. Third, we show that ASR confidence scores

2 Baseline DLM-based ASR Model 

2.1 DLM-based ASR 

We adopt Whisper-LLaDA (Wang et al., 2025) as our baseline DLM-based ASR system, using Whisper-medium.en (Radford et al., 2023) as the speech encoder and LLaDA-8B-Instruct (Nie et al., 2025) as the decoder. An FC layer projects speech representations to the DLM embedding dimension. During training, the Whisper encoder is frozen, while the DLM is updated via LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) together with the FC layer. 2.2 Block-based Decoding 

We use block-based decoding, where the output sequence is divided into blocks of size B and each block undergoes diffusion unmasking sequentially. This provides a practical speed–accuracy trade-off between AR and fully parallel decoding. Full par-allel decoding corresponds to B = L.

3.2 Target Decoding Strategies 

Based on confidence scores c(i), we select a subset of masked positions to unmask and fix for sub-sequent rounds. We evaluate three commitment schemes. 

Fixed-number (top-k) scheme. The model un-masks the k most confident tokens among the cur-rently masked positions in each round (Nie et al., 2025). 

Static confidence threshold. The model un-masks all tokens whose confidence exceeds a threshold C ∈ (0 , 1) (Yu et al., 2025). If none exceed C, the most confident token is unmasked to ensure progress.

4 Experiments 

For the main analysis metrics, we measure WER and inference speed using the Real-Time Factor (RTF), defined as the total inference time divided by the input audio duration. 

4.1 Decoding Strategies and Accuracy-Speed Trade-offs 

We compare three diffusion-based decoding strate-gies by sweeping their hyperparameters: fixed-number decoding ( k ∈ {4, 8, 16 , 32 , 64 , 128 }), static thresholding ( C ∈ {0.8, 0.9, 0.95 }), and dynamic thresholding ( f ∈ { 1.0, 0.2, 0.05 }), to-gether with two AR baselines (Fig. 1). Across all strategies, stricter commitment criteria ( i.e., smaller 

k, higher C, or lower f ) consistently reduce WER at the cost of higher RTF, revealing the accuracy– speed trade-off in diffusion-based decoding. Com-pared with fixed-number decoding, threshold-based strategies form a better Pareto frontier, achieving comparable WER with lower RTF. Static and dy-namic thresholding show similar recognition accu-racy, but static thresholding often provides lower RTF, especially in Fig. 1(b,c), making it the most balanced choice. Static thresholding also approaches the LLM-based AR baseline while offering substantial speedups. With block size 4 and C = 0 .95 , it achieves 2.81% WER, nearly matching the AR ref-erence at 2.78%, while running 1.7× faster in RTF. As block size increases, decoding becomes faster but less accurate; in the fully parallel setting, static thresholding reaches 4.13% WER while achieving a 3.5× speedup. 

4.2 Round-wise Progress Assessment 

To further analyze reliability, latency concentra-tion, and strategy-specific token commitments, we conduct a round-wise assessment. We compute progress on utterances with at least 32 text tokens, setting Pr = 1 when the first 32 tokens are fully unmasked. 

4.2.1 Prediction Reliability using Uncertainty Trajectory 

Directly measuring round-wise WER is impractical because insertion and deletion errors break align-ment across decoding rounds. We therefore use cumulative uncertainty as a proxy for prediction reliability. As shown in Fig. 2(a-c), a larger final gap from the AR reference generally corresponds to higher WER, indicating that uncertainty trajec-tories reflect recognition reliability. end, suggesting that later rounds commit more low-confidence tokens; early deviation above the AR trajectory may indicate premature incorrect com-mitments. Under matched RTF (Fig. 2(d)), static-threshold decoding tracks the AR trajectory most closely and achieves the lowest WER: 3.07% versus 4.47% for fixed-number and 3.52% for dynamic thresholding. 

4.2.2 Inference Speed using Number of Unmasked Tokens 

To identify where speed differences arise, we ana-lyze per-round token throughput, measured by the number of newly unmasked tokens per round. For a fair speed-focused comparison, we choose set-tings with comparable WERs within 3.05%–3.10%: static thresholding ( C = 0 .95 ), dynamic threshold-ing ( f = 0 .2), and fixed-number decoding ( k = 1 ). As shown in Fig. 3, static thresholding finishes in the fewest rounds on average (6.1), followed by dynamic thresholding (9.5) and fixed-number de-coding (32.0), consistent with their RTFs of 0.046, 0.081, and 0.298, respectively. The difference mainly comes from early-round token commitments. Static thresholding shows a heavy-start pattern, committing many tokens early before tapering to final refinement, whereas dy-namic thresholding proceeds more steadily and fixed-number decoding is limited by its per-round budget. These results suggest that static thresh-olding accelerates decoding by exploiting high-confidence tokens early, leaving late-stage refine-ment as the main speed bottleneck.

dence distribution and compare ASR with math reasoning on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). Specif-ically, we measure the fraction of tokens whose confidence exceeds a threshold s under parallel decoding. As shown in Fig. 4, ASR confidence scores are highly concentrated near the upper end: 93.7% of tokens satisfy s ≥ 0.90 , and 91.1% re-main above s = 0 .95 . In contrast, GSM8K shows a much more dispersed distribution, with only 59.1% and 45.7% of tokens exceeding the same thresh-olds. This likely reflects ASR’s near-monotonic speech-text alignment and unique transcription tar-get, unlike the more variable reasoning paths in GSM8K. This high-confidence skew explains the strategy-specific efficiency trends. Static thresholding exploits this profile by unmasking most high-confidence tokens early and leaving only the low-confidence tail for refinement. Dynamic thresh-olding is more conservative because its criterion can be rewritten as (k + 1)(1 − c(k)) < f , or 

c(k) > 1 − f / (k + 1) , so committing more tokens requires a threshold approaching 1. Fixed-number decoding is limited by its constant k budget and cannot exploit abundant high-confidence tokens. 

5 Conclusion 

We systematically compared fixed-number, static-threshold, and dynamic-threshold decoding for DLM-based ASR. Our analysis shows that ASR confidence scores are highly concentrated, enabling thresholding strategies, especially static threshold-ing, to commit reliable tokens early while reserving difficult positions for later refinement. These find-ings suggest that future DLM decoding strategies for ASR should focus on accelerating late-stage re-finement without sacrificing recognition accuracy. Limitations 

Our study is limited to clean read English speech from LibriSpeech test-clean. Since our finding relies on highly skewed ASR confidence scores, it may not fully generalize to noisy, spontaneous, or multilingual speech, where acoustic ambiguity, phonology, scripts, or tokenization may alter the confidence distribution and accuracy–speed trade-off. Extending the analysis to noisier benchmarks and multilingual ASR is left for future work. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work uses publicly available datasets, pre-trained models, and benchmarks, including LibriSpeech, Whisper-medium.en, LLaDA-8B-Instruct, and GSM8K, for non-commercial re-search purposes in accordance with their licenses, access conditions, and intended use. We do not col-lect new human-subject data or conduct user stud-ies. ASR systems may produce errors and should be deployed with appropriate validation and human oversight in high-stakes settings.
